3.2. Well-Being and Desire

3.2.1. Butler

Desire-Satisfaction Theory of Well-Being: Well-being turns on the satisfaction and frustration of final desire; one’s level of well-being corresponds to the total balance of desire-satisfaction over desire-frustration in one’s life.

  • Notion of Instrumental vs final desire.

  • Butler defines the following final desires:

    • self-love = final desire for one’s own well being (egoistic desires)
    • particular affections = final desires for things other than one’s own well being.

  • Butler’s Paradox of egoism: in order to achieve well-being, one must have non-egoistic desires.

    • If self-love were to “wholly engross” a person, leaving no room for particular affections, there would be absolutely nothing for that person to enjoy.
    • A person who is too focused on their own interest may be hindered from attending to the very gratifications and “disengagement” necessary to actually experience enjoyment

3.2.2. Wolf

A successful life requires meaningfulness.

Fitting Fulfillment View, which posits that “meaning arises when subjective attraction meets objective attractiveness”

  • The Subjective Element (The Fulfillment View): This view tells one to “find your passion”. To have meaning, a person must be gripped, excited, and engaged in their activities rather than feeling bored or alienated
  • The Objective Element (The Larger-than-Oneself View): Meaning also requires connecting with something whose value is independent of oneself. Wolf argues that merely feeling fulfilled is insufficient if the objects of that fulfillment are “worthless”

Both are incomplete: Wolf uses the example of “Sisyphus Fulfilled”—who is drugged into loving the futile task of rolling a stone—to show that subjective fulfillment in a pointless activity does not constitute a meaningful life. Conversely, doing objectively valuable work while feeling alienated (like a conscripted soldier or assembly-line worker) also lacks meaning .

Wolf argues that meaning is not reducible to either simple happiness (subjective) or morality (objective); instead, it requires a “suitable and inextricable link” between the two. The resolution consists of three integrated parts:

  • They must be subjectively attracted to it.
  • They must work on objectively attractive stuff.
  • They must be actively engaging with it

3.2.3. Mill

Mill recounts a “mental crisis” in 1826 where he realized that achieving his life’s goal—to be a world reformer—would not bring him happiness

  • The Anti-Self-Consciousness Theory: Mill concluded that happiness is only attained by not making it a direct end. He argued that those who are happy have their minds fixed on something other than their own happiness; by aiming at an ideal end, they find happiness “en passant”. This mirrors the “paradox of egoism” mentioned in the handouts, which suggests that focusing solely on one’s own well-being is self-defeating
  • The Culture of the Feelings: Mill realized that the “internal culture of the individual” was a prime necessity. He found that Wordsworth’s poetry provided the precise “medicine” he needed, offering a source of “inward joy” and “tranquil contemplation” that was independent of social struggle.
  • Tranquil Contemplation: Mill eventually found “real, permanent happiness in tranquil contemplation,” a discovery that aligns with Aristotle’s view of contemplation as the highest good

3.2.4. Aristotle

In the Nicomachean Ethics, Aristotle argues that the best life is one of excellent activity, defined by the concept of finality. The handout distinguishes between three grades of finality to determine the “highest good”:

  • Least Final Goods: Activities chosen only as means to an end, such as running errands or visiting the dentist
  • Partially Final Goods: Activities worth choosing for their own sake, but also for the sake of something else. Examples include morally virtuous actions, honor, or productive work
  • Final Without Qualification: Activities that are always desirable for themselves and never for the sake of anything else. Aristotle identifies happiness (eudaimonia) and contemplation in this category
