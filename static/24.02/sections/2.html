1. In “Famine, Affluence, and Morality,” Peter Singer argues there is no morally significant difference between failing to save a drowning child in a pond as you walk by and failing to help those in dire need around the world by donating to effective charities. Explain and evaluate his argument, focusing on one or two potential differences. Why might these differences be morally significant? How would Singer address them? Does his response succeed?

Difference 1 is proximity. Singer replies that you’re still capable and the victim is still suffering. Hence the moral obligation persists. Claim that the actual upstream issue is certainty.

Difference 2 is certainty. Singer responds that based on the expected value, you shoudl still give. But this weakens the “moral equivalence”. Singer responds by choosing better charities, but you may not know which ones are good. Uncertainty reduces how much you should donate. Give kelly betting as an example to motivate that risk should affect how much you weight things
