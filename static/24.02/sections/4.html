4. Objective Value

4.1. Euthyphro Dilemma

Accompanying Reading: Plato

Divine Command Theory is the philosophical view that moral truths are determined by the commands or approval of a divine being. Socrates challenges this with a famous dilemma: “Is the pious or holy loved by the gods because it is holy, or holy because it is loved by the gods?”“

Socrates and Euthyprho reject the theory and agree that: The gods love the holy because it is holy. Otherwise, it would depend on the gods opinion and be arbitrary.

So it’s merely an attribute but we haven’t defined it’s essence. Hence it must be objective.

4.2. Positivism and Relativism

Accompanying Reading: Appiah, Cosmopolitanism Chapter 2

Positivism makes a distinction between facts and values.

  • Representational States (Beliefs) can be true or false
  • Non-Representation States (Desires)
  • Positivists argue that values are just complex ways of talking about basic desires. Since these cannot be wrong or right, there are no objective moral truths.

Appiah critiques this: if we reject values because we can’t point to the facts that make them true, then we must also reject mathematical facts, logical truths, and universal claims.

4.3. Morality, Science and the Regress Problem

Acompanying Readings: Appiah, Cosmopolitanism Chapter 3

4.3.1. Regress

Acompanying Handout: Regress

  • (R1) If our ethical beliefs are justified, they are justified by inference from other beliefs.
  • (R2) Ethical beliefs cannot be justified by inference from non-ethical beliefs alone.
  • (R3) Inferential justifications cannot go on forever.
  • (R4) Inferential justifications cannot be circular.
  • (RC) Ethical beliefs cannot be justified.

Justification for R2: In order to argue from observational premises to a conclusion about unobserved reality-the idea goes—you need a “bridge premise” connecting the two.

But science faces the same exact problem. Science, like ethics, relies on unprovable assumptions. E.g. the bridge premise is that the observed is a guide to the unobserved, so that we can infer the sun will rise tomorrow.

  • (S1) If our beliefs about unobserved reality are justified, they are justified by inference from observational beliefs.
  • (S2) Beliefs about unobserved reality cannot be justified by inference from observational beliefs alone.
  • (R3) Inferential justifications cannot go on forever.
  • (R4) Inferential justifications cannot be circular. (SC) Beliefs about unobserved reality cannot be justified.

Resoluton? We can try to resist S1: we can make default assumptions without having to justify them by inference. Or resist S2: here are substantive standards of good scientific reasoning that go beyond deductive validity. Why not say the same thing about ethics?

4.3.2. Skepticism

Acompanying Handout: Morality and Science (Skepticism)

Skepticism is another hurdle to objectivity that argues we are not justified in claiming that our moral beliefs are true.

  • D1: Principle of Judgement: if you learn there is substantial disagreement over a question, you should suspend judgement
  • D2: Descriptive Relativism: There is substantial individual, cultural, and historical variation in people’s fundamental vlaues.
  • Conclusion: you should suspend judgement on questions of fundamental value

Appiah says this is unsound because D1 overgeneralizes to science. Hence D1 must be false.

  • Repair (D1*): Fundamental disagreement persists even when everyone agrees on the same evidence. If there is substantial fundamental disagreement, you should suspend judgement.

Duhem’s Thesis: In science, theories are underdetermined by evidence, meaning many different theories can be compatible with a given body of data. Yet you don’t have to suspend judgement, so Appiah argues D1* is false. Because science allows for fundamental disagreement, the fact that ethics also has fundamental disagreement does not prove that ethical truths are not objective.

  • Another repair attempt: D1** If there is substantial fundamental disagreement about a question among people who are equally good at reasoning about it, you should suspend judgement.
