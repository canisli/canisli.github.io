5.1. Consequentialism and Utilitarianism

Acompanying Readings: Shafer-Landau

Consequentialism is a family of theories asserting actions are morally required if they produce the best overall results.

The most prominent version is Act-Utilitarianism:

  1. An action is wrong iff some alternative action has a better outcome
  2. The value of an outcome is a function of well-being
  3. The function in question is: total aggregate well-being

Why not

  • Too demandign since it doesn’t give you weight over a stranger
  • Ignores distribution of well-being. Utilitarians appeal to diminishing marginal utility, e.g. log utility.
  • Mandates injustice. It’s wrong to kill an innocent person in order to save five, but not wrong to

save five people instead of one, even when the outcomes for well-being are the same.

Rule-Utilitarianism

  • An “optimific” rule set means: the set of rules whose general acceptance would produce at least as much well-being as any alternative rule set.
  • According to Rule-Utilitarianism, an act is right if and only if it conforms to an optimific set of rules.

5.2. Famine, Affluence, and Morality

Acompanying Readings: Famine, Affluence, and Morality and the 24.02 Famine, Affluence, and Morality slides

Peter Singer thought experiment: you walk past a shallow pond and see a child drowning; you can easily save the child, but doing so will muddy your clothes, which is a minor cost compared to the badness of the child’s death.

  • Singer argues for a principle: if it is in our power to prevent something very bad from happening without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we are morally obligated to do it.
  • Singer argues that physical proximity and the behavior of others are not morally relevant.

Moderate argument: We should prevent bad things unless we have to sacrifice something morally significant

Strong (more demanding) argument: We should prevent bad things unless we sacrifice something of comparable moral value

Appiah’s objection

  • Appiah argues that while the pond is a one-time emergency, global poverty is a problem that “iterates implausibly”; following Singer’s logic would eventually make the donor “nearly destitute,” which Appiah considers an unreasonable demand
  • Appiah suggests that meeting basic human needs should be the primary work of political institutions (nation-states). He argues we are obligated to contribute only our “fair share” to these collective obligations rather than carrying the whole burden alone
  • Appiah resists the idea that all human values (like art, opera, or personal projects) can be measured in a single “currency” of goodness and badness. He argues that a world where people only spent money on alleviating diarrhea would be a “flat and dreary place,” and that we are justified in being partial to our own lives and “near and dear” relationships

He instead argues for an emergency principle: If you are the person in the best position to prevent something really awful, and it won’t cost you much to do so, do it.
